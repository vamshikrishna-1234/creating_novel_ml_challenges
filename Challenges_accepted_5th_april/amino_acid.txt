1. Challenge Overview & Objective
I. Real World Context
Proteins are the functional building blocks of life, and each protein's behavior is determined by its amino acid sequence. Predicting how a protein sequence continues from a given prefix requires understanding deep biochemical patterns: hydrophobic core packing, secondary structure propensities (alpha-helix vs beta-sheet), domain boundaries, and conserved functional motifs. Pre-trained text language models treat amino acid letters as English characters and produce biologically incoherent outputs because the 20-letter amino acid alphabet follows fundamentally different statistical rules than English.

II. Objective
You are given the first 50-75% of a human protein's amino acid sequence and must predict the exact next 20 residues in the sequence. Your model must learn the "grammar" of protein sequences from scratch: which amino acids tend to follow each other, how local secondary structures constrain residue choices, and how long-range patterns (like repeating motifs) influence downstream residue selection.

III. The Training Mandate
Model Constraint: You must train a sequence model from scratch under 50 Million total parameters.
Pre-trained Weights Prohibition: Using pre-trained weights from any general-purpose language model or protein foundation model (e.g., ESM, ProtTrans, ProGen) is strictly prohibited. You must learn amino acid patterns natively from the training data.
2. Dataset Structure
The raw human proteome sequences have been processed into context/continuation pairs.

How the pairs are constructed
Each protein sequence is split at a random point between 50% and 75% of its length. The prefix becomes the context. The next 20 amino acid residues become the continuation that you must predict.

|:---|:---|:---|

| `continuation` | String | The next 20 amino acid residues in the protein sequence. This is the prediction target. |

3. Evaluation Metric: Per-Residue Accuracy
Your submission is graded using per-residue positional accuracy: each of the 20 positions in your predicted continuation is compared character-by-character against the ground truth.

Accuracy
=

Total positions across all samples

If your prediction is shorter than 20 residues, the missing positions count as incorrect. The random baseline for this metric is approximately 5% (1 out of 20 possible amino acids per position).
