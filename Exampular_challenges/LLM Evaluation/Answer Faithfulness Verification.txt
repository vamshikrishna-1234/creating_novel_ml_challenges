Overview
Large Language Models are increasingly used to answer questions about long financial documents such as annual reports, fund prospectuses, and corporate disclosures. However, LLM-generated answers are not always faithful to the source document — they may contain unsupported claims, incorrect figures, or fabricated details that sound plausible. In high-stakes financial contexts, even a single unfaithful claim can lead to serious consequences.

The Challenge
Your task is to build a faithfulness verifier for financial document question answering. Given a query about a financial document, the full document context, and an LLM-generated answer, you must predict whether the answer is faithful (supported by the context) or unfaithful (contains claims not supported by the context).

This is fundamentally a grounding verification problem: does the answer actually follow from what the document says?

Unlike typical QA evaluation benchmarks, this dataset has been carefully designed to resist surface-level shortcuts:

The dataset is length-matched — faithful and unfaithful answers have similar length distributions, so answer length alone is not a useful signal.
The task requires comparing the answer against the source context. Approaches that only analyze the answer text without referencing the context will achieve near-random performance.
Key Difficulties
Context lengths vary widely — from ~1,000 characters to ~30,000 characters. Your solution must handle both short and long financial documents without simply truncating.
Financial language is domain-specific — answers may contain precise numerical claims, legal terminology, or references to specific filing sections that must be verified against the context.
Unfaithful answers can be subtle — an answer might be mostly correct but contain one fabricated detail, a wrong number, or a misattributed claim.
No surface-level shortcuts — the dataset is designed so that simple heuristics (answer length, perplexity, readability scores) do not discriminate between faithful and unfaithful answers.
Stratified evaluation — your model is evaluated separately on short, medium, and long documents. Approaches that only optimize for the majority context length will be penalized.
Dataset
The data is provided in the dataset/ directory:

train_labeled.csv: Labeled examples with columns id, query, context, answer, label
train_unlabeled.csv: Unlabeled examples with columns id, query, context, answer
test.csv: Test data with columns id, query, context, answer
sample_submission.csv: Template for your submission
Column descriptions:

id: Unique identifier for each sample
query: A question about the financial document
context: The relevant section(s) from a financial regulatory filing. Can range from ~1K to 30K characters.
answer: An LLM-generated response to the query given the context
label: Binary target — 0 means the answer is faithful (supported by the context), 1 means the answer is unfaithful (contains unsupported claims)
Evaluation Metric
Submissions are evaluated using the Length-Stratified Harmonic Mean of ROC-AUC. This metric measures how consistently your model verifies answer faithfulness across all document lengths.

How it works:

Test samples are split into three buckets by context length:
Short: context < 5,000 characters
Medium: 5,000 <= context < 15,000 characters
Long: context >= 15,000 characters
ROC-AUC is computed separately for each bucket
Your final score is the harmonic mean of the three AUC values: Score = 3 / (1/AUC_short + 1/AUC_medium + 1/AUC_long)
Why this metric? In production financial systems, documents range from short risk-factor paragraphs to full annual reports. A faithfulness verifier that only works on short documents is unreliable. The harmonic mean ensures you cannot ignore any document-length regime — if your model achieves 0.95 AUC on long documents but only 0.55 on short ones, your score will be much lower than a model that achieves 0.80 uniformly.

A perfect model scores 1.0. Random guessing scores approximately 0.5.

Submission Format
Your submission.csv must have exactly two columns:

id: The unique identifier from test.csv
label: Your predicted probability (between 0.0 and 1.0) that the answer is unfaithful
Example:

id,label a1b2c3d4e5f6,0.15 b2c3d4e5f6g7,0.89
