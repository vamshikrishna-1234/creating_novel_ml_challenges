Dataset Description
Overview
This dataset contains paired legal-text examples created for ULP (User Legal Problems) NLP tasks.
The base text pairs originate from public settlement and litigation case records—specifically, structured summaries of class-action settlements, consumer complaints, and employment disputes compiled from legal information repositories and court documentation sources.
These records were extracted, deduplicated, and standardized into a premise–hypothesis format through a curation pipeline (text normalization, schema harmonization, and label consistency checks).
After curation, deterministic feature engineering was applied to derive cross-text targets (stance classification, legal reasoning typing, outcome inference) and surface-level statistical features (word counts, overlap ratios, linguistic markers).
Each row includes a legal premise (case summary), a user-style hypothesis statement (perspective response), and derived columns supporting NLP tasks such as NLI classification, stance detection, legal sub-issue typing, and outcome inference.

Overview
This challenge requires predicting 4 aspects of legal dispute text pairs using natural language understanding. Each test sample is a premise (legal case summary) paired with a hypothesis (user perspective statement). You must predict:

label — Natural Language Inference relationship (Entailed, Neutral, Contradict). Does the hypothesis follow from, contradict, or have no bearing on the premise?
hypothesis_stance — Fine-grained stance of the hypothesis writer toward the legal situation (~20 classes combining awareness level and emotional tone, e.g., oblivious_dismissive, informed_anxious, suspicious_uneasy). Requires understanding both what the writer knows about the situation and how they feel about it.
legal_reasoning_type — Specific legal sub-issue classification with cross-text alignment (~28 classes, e.g., biometric_collection_aligned, data_breach_implicit, robocall_violation_aligned). Requires understanding the legal violation type from the premise AND whether the hypothesis references the same domain.
case_outcome_inference — Inferred case outcome from combining premise settlement stage with hypothesis writer's relationship to the outcome (~23 classes, e.g., final_indifferent, agreed_uncertain, pending_harmed). Requires cross-text inference about resolution status and personal impact.
Why This Is Hard: These targets are not solvable from metadata or simple statistics. They require genuine NLP understanding of both texts: what happened legally (premise), what the person experienced (hypothesis), and how these relate. Surface features like word counts or settlement amounts provide marginal signal at best.

Real-World Context: The dataset represents paired legal summaries and user narratives from class-action settlements, regulatory complaints, and employment disputes. Understanding these relationships is essential for legal case triage, automated dispute analysis, and compliance risk assessment.

Evaluation
Submissions are scored using Average Macro-Averaged F1 Score across all 4 targets (higher is better, max = 1.0).

Scoring Formula:

Score = (F1_label + F1_stance + F1_legal_reasoning + F1_outcome) / 4  
Each F1 is computed independently using macro-averaging (equal weight per class, not per sample).

Why this metric?

Macro F1 penalizes models that only predict common classes — rare classes count equally.
With 20-28 classes per fine-grained target, random guessing scores ~2-5% per target.
The 4-target average means strong performance requires NLP across all tasks simultaneously.
Overall random baseline: ~10% F1. Majority-class baseline: ~5-8% F1.
from sklearn.metrics import f1_score  
import numpy as np  
  
def evaluate(y_true_list, y_pred_list):  
    scores = []  
    for y_true, y_pred in zip(y_true_list, y_pred_list):  
        all_labels = sorted(set(y_true) | set(y_pred))  
        f1 = f1_score(y_true, y_pred, labels=all_labels, average="macro", zero_division=0.0)  
        scores.append(f1)  
    return np.mean(scores)
